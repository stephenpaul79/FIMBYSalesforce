The cast of characters

Four services, one app.

FIMBY looks like a single app on your phone. Underneath, four pieces talk to each other every time you open it.

1

FIMBY Mobile App

The React Native shell you install from the App Store or Google Play. Built with Expo.

2

Auth Bridge

A small Node.js service on Vercel. The concierge between the phone and Salesforce.

3

Session Store

Upstash Redis. Remembers who's signed in so people don't re-login every 15 minutes.

4

FIMBY on Salesforce

Experience Cloud — where every feed, post, story, and message actually lives.

Step 1 · Tap the icon

You open the FIMBY app.

The app on your phone is an Expo (React Native) shell. It doesn't hold your posts or messages — it's more like a picture frame around the real FIMBY experience.

The app you installed is small on purpose. All the neighbourhood content — feed, posts, messages — comes down live from Salesforce every time you open it. That's why fixes and new features can ship the same day I make them.
Expo · React Native · WebView
Step 2 · Sign in with PKCE

You log in — but the app never sees your password.

The app opens a secure browser window to Salesforce's login page. You type your credentials there. Salesforce hands the app a temporary one-time code.

Instead of the app holding your password, Salesforce hands it a claim ticket. The ticket is useless to anyone except my server. It's the same standard your bank's app uses — it's called OAuth PKCE.
Phone ⇄ Salesforce login page
Step 3 · The Auth Bridge takes over

Vercel does the sensitive part.

The app sends that one-time code to my Auth Bridge running on Vercel. The bridge exchanges it with Salesforce for a real access token — and keeps that Salesforce token server-side. It never touches the phone.

Think of the Bridge as a train conductor. On day one you buy a 30-day pass: you punch your PIN into the station machine yourself, so the machine keeps your PIN and the conductor never sees it. The machine prints a single-use receipt; the conductor takes that receipt and hands your phone a 30-day pass in return. The pass works only on this transit line and is useless anywhere else, so a lost pass is no disaster. You punch your PIN maybe once a month, not every morning: sparing you that is the conductor's whole job.

Step 4 · Remember me

Upstash keeps you signed in for weeks.

The bridge writes a rotating refresh token into Upstash Redis. It slides for 30 days and caps out at 90 days. If someone ever tried to steal and reuse an old token, the whole family gets revoked automatically.

This is why the app doesn't nag you to log in every time. Upstash is a very fast, tiny database that just remembers "yes, this person is still signed in" for up to a month at a time — with a tripwire if anything looks off.
Upstash Redis · session store
Step 5 · Wristbands, not keys

The phone gets app-only tokens.

The bridge hands the phone two things: a 15-minute access token and a 30-day refresh token. Both are app-scoped JWTs — signed by me, useless anywhere except FIMBY. Salesforce's own credentials never leave the server.

  • Access token · 15 minutes · used on every request
  • Refresh token · 30 days sliding · used quietly to renew
  • Salesforce token · stays server-side, always
JWTs returned to the phone
Step 6 · The Frontdoor

Every session gets a fresh one-time pass.

When the phone is ready to show the actual FIMBY screens, it asks the bridge for a "frontdoor URL." The bridge signs a JWT to Salesforce, hits /singleaccess, and gets back a short-lived signed URL. The phone opens that URL in a WebView.

This is the "every day after" part. You show the conductor your 30-day pass; the conductor checks it, walks you to the platform, and unlocks the train door for that one trip. The unlock is good for only a few seconds and only for you. Even if someone grabbed it, the door would already have re-locked.
Phone → Bridge → Salesforce frontdoor
Step 7 · You're in

Salesforce Experience Cloud renders your neighbourhood.

The WebView loads the FIMBY1 Experience Cloud site. From here on, every screen you see — feed, asks & offers, stories, library, messages, notifications — is a Lightning Web Component talking to Apex on Salesforce.

This is the part that feels like the app. It's actually a live Salesforce site rendering inside your phone's browser. That's the trick that lets me ship fixes in minutes without pushing a new version to the App Store.

Bonus flow · Push notifications

When a neighbour messages you, the whole chain runs backwards.

Salesforce fires an event → the Auth Bridge relays it to Apple / Google's push service → your phone buzzes. Even if the app is closed.

Salesforce yells "new message!" to the bridge. The bridge looks up which phone belongs to that person, and asks Apple or Google to deliver the notification. All of this happens in about a second.
Why this matters

Four things this design buys us.

Zero-trust on the device

Salesforce credentials never leave the server. If a phone is lost, the wristband it holds expires in minutes and can be revoked in seconds.

Ship fixes the same day

Because the real UI lives on Salesforce Experience Cloud, I deploy and publish and neighbours see it instantly — no App Store review.

Stolen tokens self-destruct

Refresh tokens rotate. If an old one gets replayed, the whole family gets revoked automatically. That user just has to sign in again.

One codebase, two stores

The Expo shell is identical on iOS and Android. The heavy lifting is on Salesforce, so features arrive on both platforms at the same time.

That's it. Tap → sign in → wristband → frontdoor → neighbourhood. Everything else is just Salesforce doing what Salesforce does — but wrapped in something neighbours actually want to open.
