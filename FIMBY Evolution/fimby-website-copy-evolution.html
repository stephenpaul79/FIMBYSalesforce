How FIMBY's Words Changed

The app and the website are two different products. This is the story of the website's copy — how the way FIMBY introduces itself to a stranger evolved from a beautiful essay into an honest, welcoming front door.

App timeline (dates) App thinking (no dates) Website copy evolution

Scope: this tracks only the FIMBY marketing site (fimby.com). The before/after lines below are taken from the actual website edit plans. Unrelated client work that happened to share the same plans folder is not included.

Act I — The first voice

A beautiful, theology-forward essay

Late March 2026 · building out the marketing site

The first site was genuinely lovely: warm parchment, a climbing-trellis metaphor, a Vision page written like lay theology, and a closing litany — "Learn a name. Share a loaf. Offer a ride." It led with worldview and trusted the reader to stay for the product.

It worked beautifully for one kind of visitor. The question was whether it worked for everyone walking past.

Act II — The reckoning

A market-research read through many eyes

March 29, 2026 · multi-demographic perception audit

Before changing a word, the site was read the way a dozen different strangers would read it — by age, faith, culture, income, sexuality, and more. The diagnosis had a name:

The core insight: a funnel mismatch

The product (lending, bulk buys, events, consent-first design, no metrics) has broad, cross-demographic appeal. The framing (church affiliation, literary prose, English-only) quietly narrowed it.

The site was strongest when it showed the app and weakest when it explained the worldview — and the worldview was front-loaded on the inner pages.

The audit named the specific places trust leaked out:

  • The church connection surfaced too late, so some visitors felt it as a reveal rather than an introduction
  • The Vision page was written for educated, reflective readers and lost almost everyone else
  • An unaddressed LGBTQ+ trust gap sat next to a denomination with a mixed record
  • No acknowledgement of the real Downtown Eastside context the project grew in
  • English-only, in a neighbourhood with large Cantonese, Mandarin, and Vietnamese populations
  • No social proof — "is anyone actually on this?"
Act III — Building trust, line by line

The trust-friction edits

March 29, 2026 · concrete, file-by-file copy changes

The fix wasn't to hide the soul of the project — that would be dishonest, and the honesty was a strength. It was to let every visitor meet the practical value, and the founder's real story, before the theology. The clearest example is the footer:

Footer attribution — from sponsor logo to origin story
Before
"An initiative of Strathcona Vineyard"
After
"Made by neighbours in Vancouver's Downtown Eastside, supported by Strathcona Vineyard"

The most sensitive trust questions — church, LGBTQ+, neighbourhood authenticity — all had the same answer hiding in plain sight: the founder's own life. So the copy stopped explaining the church away and started telling the truth plainly.

New FAQ added
Is FIMBY safe for LGBTQ+ people?
"Yes. FIMBY's founder is a gay man who has been part of Strathcona Vineyard for years, and FIMBY is built for all neighbours. That means all neighbours. You are welcome here exactly as you are. No one is asked to hide who they are in order to belong."
"Who built this?" — from institution-first to neighbour-first
Before
"FIMBY was created by Stephen Rathjen and the community of Strathcona Vineyard, a church in Vancouver's Downtown Eastside…"
After
"FIMBY was created by Stephen Rathjen, a neighbour who has lived in Vancouver's Downtown Eastside for more than 20 years. It grew out of real neighbouring before it became an app: shared meals, lending tools, prayer, and showing up for one another…"
New section — Our Approach
"Forged in a real neighbourhood"
"FIMBY was shaped in Vancouver's Downtown Eastside, a neighbourhood marked by beauty, resilience, addiction, overdose, displacement, and deep community. We do not pretend an app can fix those things. But we have seen what changes when people know one another by name."

Three more moves rounded out the pass: a crisis boundary FAQ ("FIMBY is not a replacement for shelters, detox, health care, or crisis lines"), a Community Groups story so local organizations could be a bridge to formal supports, and new mock cards showing elders and diverse neighbours offering, not only being helped — like Arthur T.'s "tomato starts, free to a good garden."

Act IV — Saying it for real

Legal pages & a pre-launch edit pass

Late April 2026 · making the words match the app

With the app nearly ready for the stores, the website had to become accountable. Terms of service, privacy, community standards, and a delete-account page were drafted and published — and a full edit pass swept the marketing copy so that every claim matched a feature that actually existed in the live product.

Act V — From "lovely" to "what do I do?"

The engagement & conversion audit

June 2026 · conversion-focused, as launch approached

As visitors began arriving at the site, a new question replaced "is it beautiful?": does a stranger know what to do in ten seconds? An internal north star was set — and a guardrail to keep the warmth.

Internal north star (never shown to visitors)

FIMBY is local social resilience infrastructure — a "build trust before you need it" platform. Resilience through relationship; measure trust, not engagement; make receiving as normal as giving; scale like yeast.

Anti-fear guardrail: no collapse, disruption, or prepper framing in public copy. Fear builds bunkers; love builds arks; the door stays open.

The biggest structural change: stop making every visitor feel the site was only for one neighbourhood. The first screen forked into two equal welcomes.

"I live in an active neighbourhood"

Check your postal code and join.

Join

"I want FIMBY where I live"

Bring FIMBY — launch a hub, with a waitlist that captures intent.

Bring FIMBY

Alongside the fork: a scannable capability strip for readers who don't study app mockups, Support People lifted out of the FAQ into a homepage trust block, success reframed around trust indicators instead of vanity metrics, the mock UI refreshed to match the now-live app, and repeated signature phrases ("fimbyisms") de-duplicated so each one keeps its punch.

What the words learned

The website's copy didn't get less honest over time — it got more honest, earlier. The theology stayed; it just stopped being the bouncer at the door. The founder's real life moved to the front. And the practical value of being known by your neighbours led, on every page, before the worldview that inspired it.

Show the app. Tell the truth. Lead with the welcome.
