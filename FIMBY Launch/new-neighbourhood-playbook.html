The premise: small is the strategy

Most social apps fail cold start because they need thousands of users before they're worth opening. FIMBY needs about twenty — in one geography, who can recognize each other. That's the whole game: assemble one atomic network of 15–30 neighbours, make it feel alive within weeks, and let trust do the growing.

Which means a FIMBY launch is not a marketing campaign. It's closer to organizing a really good block party — and then not letting the momentum die.

A neighbourhood doesn't join FIMBY because it downloaded an app.
It joins because someone local decided their block should know each other, and did the work.

Three rules travel with every launch:

  • Offline first. The relationship precedes the app. Recruitment, welcome, and recovery all happen face to face; the app documents and extends what's already real.
  • Protect the first ask. An unanswered ask is a trust wound. In a new neighbourhood's first months, someone owns making sure no ask ever goes quiet.
  • Cadence beats volume. One weekly post the neighbourhood can count on beats ten sporadic ones. Predictability is what makes a small network feel alive.
The launch kit

Six ingredients, no substitutions

A neighbourhood launches when all six exist — not when enough people are curious. Missing ingredients are recruited, not waived.

1 · An anchor host

One local person who owns the launch: convener, welcomer, first responder. They live there, they're known, and they've got roughly 4–6 hours a week for six months. Not a FIMBY staffer parachuted in.

The kit fails without this

2 · A core of 10–20 neighbours

People the anchor host can name and recognize, each personally invited. This list is the launch — posters and press come later, if ever.

Names, not estimates

3 · Two orgs on a rhythm

Churches, neighbourhood houses, community centres — each committed to one recurring post (a meal, a drop-in, a ride offer). Orgs are the feed's steady pulse when peer activity is quiet.

Committed, not just supportive

4 · A moderator

Someone who activates accounts within 24 hours and gives every newcomer a human welcome. Often the anchor host at first — but named, and with FIMBY team support behind them.

5 · A seeded library & first bulk buy

About 30 lendable items (every core member lists two or three) and one Costco-run bulk buy with a few committed participants. Single-player value that works before trust exists.

6 · A standing gathering

One recurring, low-key event — soup night, movie night, porch coffee — with a named host. Gatherings are the launch engine: each one generates a week of real feed content.

Also count the offline neighbours. FIMBY's support-person model means one account can carry elders or low-tech neighbours into the neighbourhood's life. Recruit supported participants deliberately — a 20-account launch can be a 35-person network.
The gate

Go / no-go: the pre-launch checklist

Run this the month before opening signups. Every line true, or the date moves. A delayed launch costs nothing; a dead hub is expensive and hard to restart.

  • ✓The anchor host has done a seeding gathering and the core group signed up shoulder-to-shoulder.In-person signup surfaces every onboarding rough edge while help is standing right there.
  • ✓At least 10 core members are active — each has listed items, RSVP'd, or joined the bulk buy.Low-vulnerability actions first; asking for help comes after trust.
  • ✓Both orgs have already posted on their rhythm — before launch, not promised for after.
  • ✓One full loop has closed end-to-end: ask → response → help happened → thanks posted, by name.The feed must prove that asking works here before strangers arrive to test it.
  • ✓Every core member is vouched and the vouch flow has been practiced.
  • ✓Moderator activation runs in under 24 hours, with a personal welcome attached.
  • ✓The launch gathering has a date, a place, and food — and core members each know which neighbour they're bringing.
The arc

Five phases, six months

Every neighbourhood so far follows the same shape. Dates flex; the order and the exit tests don't. (The V6A timeline file holds the detailed month-by-month checklists behind these phases.)

Phase 1 — Seed

≈ two months before launch

Goal: assemble the kit. Entirely offline.

  • Anchor host writes the list of 10–20 and invites each one personally — face to face, never a group text.
  • Helpers get small named roles (greeter, library steward, gathering host). A role is easier to say yes to than "help me".
  • Org rhythms locked: the post, the day, the person.
  • Seeding gathering held; library seeded to ~30 items; first bulk buy planted.
Exit test: the six kit ingredients all exist, by name.

Phase 2 — Rehearse

≈ one month before launch

Goal: the core group builds habits, and every loop runs end-to-end at least once before an audience arrives.

  • First on-app gathering: posted, RSVP'd, held, and thanked afterward by name.
  • 2–3 safe first asks planted and answered within hours.
  • Onboarding dry-run with people who missed the seeding gathering; every confusion noted.
  • Week-one content calendar sketched — real things, scheduled so they don't clump.
Exit test: the go / no-go gate above passes in full.

Phase 3 — Open the doors

Launch month

Goal: newcomers walk into a room where something is already happening. Highest-touch month of the six.

  • Launch gathering first, broad signups second — the doorway is a table, not a poster.
  • Zero-unanswered-asks guarantee: the anchor host sweeps the feed twice daily and hand-routes anything stalling ("Marcus has a truck — I'll text him").
  • Every newcomer welcomed within 24 hours and connected to someone who can vouch.
  • Every newcomer reaches one real action in week one: borrow, RSVP, or join a bulk buy.
Exit test: weekly rhythm unbroken; every ask answered; new members acting, not just scrolling.

Phase 4 — Hand over

Months 1–2 after launch

Goal: habits replace launch energy, and the anchor host starts giving the job away.

  • Second wave recruited through members, not marketing: "who's one neighbour you'd vouch for?"
  • Standing gathering gets a host who isn't the anchor.
  • One moderation duty handed to a helper; quiet members get a doorstep hello.
  • First reciprocity pass: only-givers invited to receive, only-receivers offered a way to give.
  • One deliberate week without hand-routing asks — the stalls show exactly where the network is still thin.
Exit test: at least half the weekly rhythm happens without the anchor host pushing.

Phase 5 — Prove & record

Months 3–4 after launch

Goal: demonstrate the neighbourhood runs without its founder, and bank the lessons.

  • The succession test: anchor host steps fully back for two weeks. Rhythm holds? Asks answered? Gatherings happen?
  • A thanksgiving gathering celebrates the loops closed since launch, by name.
  • The operator's log becomes feedback into this playbook: what to repeat, skip, or change.
Exit test: the "alive" dashboard below reads green — then, and only then, is this neighbourhood a template for the next one.
Division of labour

Who does what

FIMBY grows one neighbourhood at a time because each launch needs local ownership. The split of responsibilities:

RoleOwns
Anchor host (local)The list of 10–20, personal invitations, the launch and standing gatherings, the zero-unanswered-asks guarantee, the operator's log.
Moderator (local)Account activation within 24 hours, personal welcomes, connecting newcomers to a voucher. Often the anchor host at first.
Org partners (local)One recurring post each, on a named day, by a named person. The feed's steady pulse.
Helpers (local)Small named roles: greeter, library steward, gathering host. Sized to be kept, not heroic.
FIMBY teamThe platform, onboarding materials, moderator training and backup, this playbook, and the go / no-go call alongside the anchor host.
Sizing rule for helpers: when a helper goes quiet, their role is probably too big. Shrink it before they disappear — a smaller kept commitment beats a bigger abandoned one.
Health, not hype

How we know a neighbourhood is alive

FIMBY's purpose is relationship outside the app, so health is measured in loops closed and reciprocity — never attention. By month four post-launch:

MeasureHealthy signal
Ask fulfillmentAt least 90% of asks answered within 48 hours, without hand-routing.
Loops closedLoans completed and returned, bulk buys fulfilled, gatherings held — rising month over month.
ReciprocityHalf of active members have both given and received. Nobody stays the helper; nobody stays the helped.
Repeat pairsThe same two neighbours interacting a second and third time — the earliest signal of real relationship.
Vouch coverageEveryone vouched; no one stuck at the borrowing gate.
SuccessionTwo weeks without the anchor host, and the rhythm holds.

Deliberately not measured: daily actives, session length, post counts. If those become the scoreboard, the neighbourhood has lost the plot.

Discipline

When to say "not yet"

The waitlist tells us where people want FIMBY. The kit tells us where it can actually live. Expansion is a supply decision, not a demand decision — these are the honest reasons to hold a launch:

  • No anchor host. Enthusiastic signups without a local owner produce a hub nobody tends. Recruit the host first; everything else follows them.
  • A list of "interested people" instead of a list of names. If the host can't name 10–20 neighbours they recognize, the seeding phase hasn't happened yet.
  • Orgs supportive but not committed. A letter of support doesn't post weekly meals. Wait for the rhythm to actually start.
  • The launch would depend on the FIMBY team being present. If it can't run locally, it can't run. The team supports; it doesn't substitute.
  • The previous neighbourhood isn't green yet. Each launch consumes the team's concierge capacity. A struggling hub plus a new launch usually means two struggling hubs.
Why this matters: a dead hub is worse than a waitlist. A waitlisted neighbour stays hopeful; a neighbour who joined a silent feed becomes evidence for "another dead app" — and wins that argument on your behalf.

FIMBY can't make anyone a good neighbour.
A launch just gives good neighbouring somewhere to grow.
