Load the cannon, then fire it once

The bigger-bang plan: quiet founder-sized prep, then one church-hosted neighbourhood launch night that puts a recognizable crowd on the app in a single evening — so nobody arrives to tumbleweeds. The gentle one-thing-a-week starter stays as your low-energy fallback.

Why a bang — and why it isn't on your shoulders

A neighbourhood feed has a liquidity threshold: below it, every newcomer opens the app, sees nothing, and leaves — and you only get one first impression each. A slow drip of one-coffee-a-week keeps joins staggered, so everyone arrives to emptiness. That's the tumbleweed death you're worried about, and you're right to be.

The fix isn't you grinding harder. It's a lot of people who already know each other joining in the same evening — so each one opens the app and instantly sees familiar faces.

The density comes from the church and the block, not from you: Strathcona Vineyard is a pre-assembled, trusting, V6A-rooted crowd, and a leader's public blessing confers permission in one night that a hundred of your coffees couldn't. Your job is to load the cannon — team, church buy-in, seeded content — and then let a bounded, team-run event do the loud part.

And a dated event is actually easier on your fluctuating energy than an endless daily drip: it has edges. You prepare for one evening with a team, then hand off — instead of being on call forever.

Design targets, not your scoreboard

Phase 1 · Load the cannon

Quiet prep, founder-sized

≈ 5–6 weeks · one-thing-a-week pace

No public anything yet. This is the gentle starter's work, pointed at one evening. Most items are a single warm conversation with someone you already know — couch-week fallbacks still apply (a text instead of a meeting).

Green/couch still applies here. These weeks are gentle by design. On a low week, do the text-version of an item or a solo task (seeding your own library items), and let a teammate carry a conversation. Nothing is public yet, so nobody's waiting.
Phase 2 · Launch night

One loud evening — team-run

the bang · a single dated event

Church-hosted, block-aimed. Everyone joins in the same window and posts their first thing in the room, so the feed fills live and each person walks out already seeing neighbours they recognize. Your personal load: about five minutes at the front, then circulate. The team runs the rest — so it survives even if the night lands on a low day.

Run of show (~75 minutes)

  • 0–15 min
    Food and mingling. Warm, low-key, name tags. People arrive to a room, not a presentation.
  • 15–25 min
    Welcome + blessing. A Vineyard leader introduces and blesses it; you tell the one true story (the neighbour who ended up inside another's home after nine years a block apart). Five minutes, not a pitch.
  • 25–45 min
    Sign up together. Team works the signup station; everyone joins at once. This synchronized join is the whole point.
  • 45–60 min
    Post your first thing. Everyone lists one ask, offer, or lendable item right now. The feed fills to ~30 posts live, in front of them.
  • 60–75 min
    Name the next beat. Announce the first gathering / bulk buy from the front so there's an obvious next thing, then back to mingling.
Phase 3 · Catch, don't carry

Turn one night into a habit

first 2–3 weeks after · team on a rota

The danger after a bang is a founder-load spike — a surge of welcomes and questions right when you might hit a couch week. So the team catches it, on a rota, and the promise is "no neighbour is ignored," not "Stephen answers everything."

Expect a week-two dip. The post-event lull is normal, not failure. Answer it with the second gathering, not a guilt spiral — and let the team, not you alone, hold the line.
A bang is one-shot

Fire safely

You can't cheaply re-bang, so a handful of conditions are non-negotiable before you light the fuse:

  • Never fire an empty cannon. Seed content and the team must be live before the night. An empty feed on launch night spends your one best impression.
  • Keep it walkable. Aim the invite at a geographic core. A scattered city-wide crowd feels dead to each person even if the headcount is high — and it breaks the neighbourhood trust boundary.
  • Make it survive your low day. Team-run and church-hosted means the night happens even if you're depleted. You owe five minutes at the front; the rest has other hands.
  • The prep earns the bang. If Phase 1 isn't done, move the date. A steady later night beats a frantic empty one — and delay costs far less than a dead-on-arrival launch.
Your part, kept human

What's actually on you

The bang is bigger, but your share stays bounded — because the church and the team carry the density and the surge. Three rules from the starter carry straight over:

  • Never miss twice. Skip a prep week and just don't skip the next. Reduce the next action; don't rewrite the plan.
  • If a step demands the tea-and-cookies you, shrink it or move it to a green week. A wave is not a broken promise; a text is not a failed meeting.
  • Bounded by default. The launch is one dated, finite evening with a run-of-show and a team — edges are exactly what make a big moment possible for someone with up-and-down energy.
Your whole scoreboard is unchanged: "Did I take one honest, bounded action this week?" The takeoff numbers above describe the event, not you. The church fills the room; you just keep loading the cannon, one bounded action at a time.
