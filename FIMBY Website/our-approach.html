Our approach

Belonging Trust Local Quiet Ownership Roots vs. Facebook

You can put it down

We're more connected than ever, and somehow lonelier for it. Somewhere along the way, the places we went to feel close to people started asking us to perform, to optimize, to keep scrolling, and quietly sold what they learned about us along the way.

FIMBY is built backwards from all of that. No audience to play to. No algorithm deciding what you see. No one keeping score. Just the people who actually live near you, and small, real ways to show up for each other.

A borrowed pot becomes a conversation.
A shared meal becomes a check-in.
That happens off the screen, which is the whole point.

Belonging over
performance

No likes. No followers.
No trending.

FIMBY has no engagement metrics, no algorithm optimizing for time-on-screen, and no follower counts. There's no audience to perform for.

Instead, you keep crossing paths with the same people, familiar faces first, real relationships after.

Trust travels through people,
not ratings

Online, trust is a number: a star rating, a review count, a score that trails you everywhere and whose math you never actually see. FIMBY doesn't work that way.

Before you borrow something from a neighbour, someone who already knows you vouches: a simple “I know this person, they're a good egg.” Not a credit check. Not a background investigation. The kind of introduction neighbours have always made for each other.

And there's no public scoreboard. No neighbour browsing your profile can see who vouched for you, and there's no wall of stars beside anyone's name. Vouching carries quiet weight precisely because it stays personal.

You're not trusting a stranger's rating.
You're trusting a neighbour's word.

The same care shows up in what you share. Contact details are never handed out automatically. Sharing is granular and per-field, and you see exactly what the other person will see before anything leaves your hands. Blocking is bidirectional and never forces re-contact. Leaving is a right, with a 30-day grace window if you change your mind.

And when something goes sideways, FIMBY reaches for restoration, not punishment. Even the words are chosen to assume the best:

Small enough to recognize
each other

FIMBY is scoped to your immediate neighbourhood, not a city-wide feed, not a region. Just the people around you.

When a neighbourhood gets too big, it splits into smaller nearby neighbourhoods, like a healthy cell dividing. Each one stays small enough that you know who's who. If it ever becomes a city-wide contact list instead of a neighbourhood, it has failed.

Belonging starts the moment
the faces stop being strangers.

This is intentional. A neighbourhood that stays legible is a neighbourhood that stays safe, warm, and useful. Everything in FIMBY is designed to keep it that way.

And no one is shut out for being new to this. You don't have to be online to belong: someone you trust can take part on your behalf, and a parent can carry a profile for a child. The neighbourhood gets to know the name; the person stays cared for.

When should FIMBY rest?

Late at night, we rest: no buzzes, no pings.
Your neighbours' messages will be here in the morning.

A doorbell,
not a slot machine

FIMBY's notification system is intentionally quiet. You set quiet hours during onboarding, choose which categories you want to hear about, and FIMBY batches the rest into a daily digest.

FIMBY is designed for brief check-ins, not endless scrolling.

Even loading screens have personality: neighbourhood metaphors instead of spinners. Because if your app has to make you wait, it should at least make you smile.

FIMBY is resting right now,
but you're always welcome.

Community-owned,
not ad-driven

No ads. No data harvesting. No engagement metrics. FIMBY doesn't sell your attention or your information.

It's a non-profit endeavour, sustained by donations, grants, and community contributions, governed by neighbours, and built to serve the people who use it, not the people who want to reach them.

Your data is not the product.
Your neighbours are not an audience to be monetized.

Forged in a real neighbourhood

FIMBY was shaped in Vancouver's Downtown Eastside, a neighbourhood marked by beauty, resilience, addiction, displacement, poverty, and deep community. Twenty years of neighbouring there taught one thing plainly: the people closest to you are often the ones who keep you going. An app can't fix what ails a block, but we've seen what changes when people know one another by name.

Where money is tight, that kind of closeness isn't only warm. It's practical. A borrowed drill, a split bulk-buy, a skill shared instead of hired: small exchanges that let a household lean on its neighbours instead of going without. Sharing what's already on the block is one of the oldest ways a neighbourhood has held itself together.

It grew out of a church community that learned faith looks like showing up for people unlike you, so FIMBY is open to everyone. Some neighbours share a prayer request because that's part of their life; no one is ever expected to join in. Local churches, centres, and community groups have a place here too, sharing meals, events, and steady help, so informal care and formal supports sit a little closer together.

FIMBY can't make anyone a good neighbour.
It just gives good neighbouring somewhere to grow.
